Glassmorphism emerged as a dominant UI trend in the early 2020s, drawing inspiration from Apple's macOS Big Sur and Microsoft's Fluent Design System. The style creates an illusion of frosted glass floating above colorful backgrounds, producing interfaces that feel simultaneously modern and ethereal. Its hallmark is the use of CSS `backdrop-filter: blur()` combined with semi-transparent backgrounds and subtle border highlights.

## When to Use

Glassmorphism excels in dashboards, music players, portfolio sites, and any application where you want to create a sense of depth and premium feel without heavy visual weight. It works particularly well over gradient or image-rich backgrounds where the blur effect can truly shine. Avoid using it for text-heavy interfaces or contexts where maximum readability is critical, as the transparency can reduce contrast.

## Key Visual Elements

The core visual ingredients are semi-transparent panels (typically `rgba(255, 255, 255, 0.1)` to `0.25`), a backdrop blur of 10-20px, and a thin 1px border in `rgba(255, 255, 255, 0.2)` to catch light on the panel edges. Layering multiple glass panels at different depths creates a compelling parallax-like effect. Background gradients using the primary and secondary colors provide the colorful canvas that makes the frosted effect visible.

## Typography

Pair geometric sans-serif headings like Poppins with the clean neutrality of Inter for body text. Keep font weights on the lighter side (300-500) to complement the airy feel of the glass panels. White or near-white text on dark translucent backgrounds ensures readability while maintaining the ethereal aesthetic.

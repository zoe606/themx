Art Deco emerged in the 1920s and 1930s as a celebration of modernity, luxury, and geometric beauty. From the Chrysler Building to the posters of Cassandre, it combined machine-age precision with opulent materials and bold symmetry. In digital design, Art Deco brings a sense of timeless glamour and architectural grandeur that immediately communicates sophistication and prestige.

## When to Use

Art Deco excels for luxury hotels and restaurants, upscale event websites, premium brand identities, cocktail bars and speakeasy-themed venues, fashion and jewelry brands, and any project that wants to evoke the glamour of the Jazz Age. It also works well for wedding websites, architectural firm portfolios, and cultural institutions with historical connections. The style is inherently dramatic and best suited for experiences rather than utility-focused interfaces.

## Key Visual Elements

Symmetry and geometry are paramount. Expect sunburst patterns, chevrons, fan shapes, and stepped architectural forms. Gold and brass tones contrast against deep navy, black, or emerald backgrounds. Ornamental borders frame content sections, often incorporating repeating geometric motifs. Vertical lines and elongated proportions create a sense of height and aspiration. The palette typically centers on metallic gold, deep dark backgrounds, and carefully selected jewel-tone accents.

## Typography

Poiret One is an Art Deco classic with its geometric construction, circular O's, and elegant thin strokes. Raleway provides a complementary geometric sans-serif for body text that maintains the era's aesthetic while ensuring modern readability. Use generous letter-spacing (0.1-0.2em) for headings, and consider all-caps treatments for navigation and labels. Decorative initial caps or drop caps can add period-appropriate ornamentation.

The Swiss (or International Typographic) Style originated in Switzerland and Germany in the 1950s and remains one of the most influential design movements in history. Rooted in principles of cleanliness, readability, and objectivity, it treats the grid as a near-sacred organizing principle. Every element has a mathematically justified position, yet the results feel effortlessly composed rather than rigid.

## When to Use

Swiss-International style is ideal for editorial websites, corporate identities, data-heavy publications, and any project where clarity and professionalism are paramount. It suits brands that want to communicate authority and trustworthiness through restraint. The approach also scales beautifully -- the same grid system works for a business card and a billboard.

## Key Visual Elements

The grid is the foundation of everything. Typically a multi-column grid (6, 9, or 12 columns) dictates placement of all elements. Color is used sparingly -- often just black, white, and a single accent color (traditionally red). Photography is presented cleanly without filters or overlays. Generous whitespace is not empty space but an active design element that gives content room to breathe and creates visual rhythm.

## Typography

Helvetica Neue (or its close relatives like Aktiv Grotesk and Inter) is the canonical choice. Type hierarchy is established through dramatic differences in scale rather than decorative treatments. A headline might be 72px while body text sits at 16px, creating clear visual levels. Flush-left, ragged-right alignment is preferred over justified text.

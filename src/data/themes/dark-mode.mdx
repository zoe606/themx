Dark mode design moved from a niche developer preference to a mainstream expectation around 2020 when every major operating system and platform adopted system-wide dark themes. Far more than simply inverting colors, well-executed dark mode requires a carefully constructed elevation system where lighter surface shades convey hierarchy against a near-black base.

## When to Use

Dark mode is appropriate for virtually any application, from media players and code editors to corporate dashboards and social platforms. It is especially effective for content-consumption interfaces, low-light environments, and OLED screens where true blacks save battery. Offering dark mode as a user preference alongside a light theme has become a baseline expectation for modern products.

## Key Visual Elements

The background is never pure black (#000000) — a very dark gray such as #121212 reduces harsh contrast and avoids the "infinity void" effect. Surfaces at higher elevations use progressively lighter grays (#1E1E1E, #2C2C2C, #383838) to simulate light accumulation. Primary and secondary colors are desaturated compared to their light-theme counterparts so they remain legible without causing glare. Borders are used minimally; elevation and subtle tonal shifts handle separation.

## Typography

A single versatile typeface like Inter works across both headings and body in dark mode, where legibility depends more on weight contrast than font variety. Use semi-bold or bold for headings and regular weight for body text. Avoid pure white (#FFFFFF) for body copy — a slightly muted tone like #E1E1E1 prevents halation and improves long-reading comfort.

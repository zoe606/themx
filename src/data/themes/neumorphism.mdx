Neumorphism — also known as soft UI — emerged around 2020 as a stylistic evolution bridging flat design and skeuomorphism. It relies entirely on carefully tuned box shadows to create the illusion that interface elements are extruded from or pressed into a single continuous surface. The result feels tactile yet minimal, like molded plastic or clay.

## When to Use

Neumorphism works best for personal dashboards, music players, smart-home controls, and lifestyle apps where an understated, premium feel is desired. Because the effect depends on a uniform light-colored background, it suits interfaces with limited color variety and focused interaction areas. Avoid it for text-heavy or data-dense screens where accessibility demands stronger contrast.

## Key Visual Elements

The defining feature is the dual-shadow technique: a light shadow (usually white or near-white) on one side and a darker shadow on the opposite side simulate a single directional light source. Backgrounds, cards, and buttons share the same base hue, differing only in shadow direction and intensity. Borders are almost never used — depth and separation come exclusively from shadows. Pressed states simply invert the shadow direction, creating a concave impression.

## Typography

Choose rounded, friendly sans-serif typefaces. Poppins works well for headings with its geometric warmth, while Rubik provides comfortable readability for body text. Keep font weights moderate — ultra-bold type competes with the subtle shadow aesthetic. Use the text color sparingly against the light background, relying on size and weight rather than color for hierarchy.
